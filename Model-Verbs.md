***What are modal verbs.***

Modal verbs are a special category of auxiliary (helping) verbs.
Such as 
* Can
* Could
* May
* Might
* Must
* Shall
* Should
* Will
* Would

Common Modal Verbs:

* Ability/Permission: Can, Could
* Possibility/Probability: May, Mighy, Could, Should.
* Obligation/Necessity: Must, Should, Ought to.
* Future/Conditional: Will, Would, Shall.

Examples in Context.

* Ability: I can speak Spanish.
* Permission: Could I use your phone?
* Necessity: You must submit the report today.
* Possibility: it might snow tomorrow.
* Advice: You should eat more vegetables.


***German Modal verbs (Modalverben)***

German modal verbs are a special group of six essesntial verbs:

* Können 
* Müssen 
* Dürfen 
* Sollen
* Wollen
* Mögen


* Können: ( can/to be able to): Ability or possibility ("Ich kann schwimmen")
* Müssen: (must/to have to): Necessity or obligation ("Ich muss arbeiten")
* Dürfen: (may/to be allowed to): Permission or Prohibition ("Ich darf hier parken")
* Sollen: (Should/ought to): Obligation, advice, or rumor ("Ich soll mehr trinken")
* Wollen: (Want to/intent): Desire or plan ("Ich will nach Berlin").
* Mögen: (to like): Perference or fondness ("Ich mag Schokolade).

Note: Möchten(Would like) is techincally the subjunctive form of mögen but is commony treated as a 7th modal verb.

Key Grammatical Features:

* Word Order: The modal verb is conjugated in position II, while the main verb goes to the very end in its infinitive form.
  Example: Ich kann Deutsch sprechen.

* Conjugation Rules: In the present tense, the ich(I) and er/es/sie (he/it/she) forms are identical and have no ending, while the vowel often changes from the infinitive.

* Sentence Structure: In subordinate clauses, the modal verb moves to the end of the clause.

Examples:

* Ich kann heute nicht kommen - I cannot come today.

* Du darfst das nicht tun - You are not allowed to do that.

* Soll ich dir helfen? - Should I help you.
